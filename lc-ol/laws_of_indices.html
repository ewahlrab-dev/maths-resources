Laws of Indices

Section 2.7  —  Eight rules every student must know

What is an index? In the expression \(2^3 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8\), the number 3 is called the index (or power). It tells us how many times the base (2) is multiplied by itself. The eight rules below show how indices behave when we multiply, divide, and combine them.
1
Multiplication
To multiply powers of the same base, add the indices.
\[ x^m \times x^n = x^{m+n} \]
Worked Examples
  • 1 \(x^2 \times x^3 = x^{2+3}\) → \(x^5\)
  • 2 \(x^4 \times x^6 = x^{4+6}\) → \(x^{10}\)
  • 3 \(x^3 \times x = x^{3+1}\) → \(x^4\)  (remember: \(x = x^1\))
Watch out: This rule only works when the base is the same. You cannot simplify \(x^2 \times y^3\) any further.
2
Division
To divide powers of the same base, subtract the indices.
\[ x^m \div x^n = x^{m-n} \]
Worked Examples
  • 1 \(x^5 \div x^2 = x^{5-2}\) → \(x^3\)
  • 2 \(x^8 \div x^3 = x^{8-3}\) → \(x^5\)
  • 3 \(x^4 \div x^4 = x^{4-4} = x^0\) → \(1\)  (links to Rule 5)
3
Power to a Power
To raise a power to a further power, multiply the indices.
\[ (x^m)^n = x^{m \times n} \]
Worked Examples
  • 1 \((x^2)^3 = x^{2 \times 3}\) → \(x^6\)
  • 2 \((x^4)^3 = x^{4 \times 3}\) → \(x^{12}\)
  • 3 \((x^5)^2 = x^{5 \times 2}\) → \(x^{10}\)
Common mistake: Students sometimes add instead of multiply. \((x^2)^3\) is NOT \(x^5\) — it is \(x^6\).
4
A Product Raised to a Power
When a product is raised to a power, each factor gets that power.
\[ (ab)^n = a^n \times b^n \]
Worked Examples
  • 1 \((2x)^3 = 2^3 \times x^3\) → \(8x^3\)
  • 2 \((3x^2)^2 = 3^2 \times x^4\) → \(9x^4\)
  • 3 \((2xy)^4 = 2^4 \times x^4 \times y^4\) → \(16x^4y^4\)
5
Any Number to the Power of Zero is 1
No matter what the base is, raising it to the power zero always gives 1.
\[ a^0 = 1 \]
Worked Examples
  • 1 \(10^0\) → \(1\)
  • 2 \(x^0\) → \(1\)
  • 3 \((3ab)^0\) → \(1\)
  • 4 \(5 \times x^0 = 5 \times 1\) → \(5\)  (only the \(x^0\) becomes 1)
Why? From Rule 2: \(x^4 \div x^4 = x^0\). But any number divided by itself = 1. So \(x^0 = 1\).
6
Negative Indices
A negative index means take the reciprocal (flip it).
\[ a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n} \]
Worked Examples
  • 1 \(x^{-2} = \dfrac{1}{x^2}\)
  • 2 \(2^{-3} = \dfrac{1}{2^3} = \dfrac{1}{8}\) → \(\dfrac{1}{8}\)
  • 3 \(4^{-2} = \dfrac{1}{4^2} = \dfrac{1}{16}\) → \(\dfrac{1}{16}\)
  • 4 \(\dfrac{1}{x^3}\) can be written as → \(x^{-3}\)
Tip: Moving a power from the bottom of a fraction to the top (or vice versa) changes the sign of the index.
7
Fractional Indices
A fractional index with 1 on top means take a root.
\[ x^{\frac{1}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{x} \]
Why? A quick proof for \(x^{\frac{1}{2}}\)
  • 1 Using Rule 1: \(x^{\frac{1}{2}} \times x^{\frac{1}{2}} = x^{\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2}} = x^1 = x\)
  • 2 But also: \(\sqrt{x} \times \sqrt{x} = x\)
  • 3 Therefore \(x^{\frac{1}{2}} = \sqrt{x}\) ✓
Example A
\(25^{\frac{1}{2}} = \sqrt{25}\) → 5
Example B
\(8^{\frac{1}{3}} = \sqrt[3]{8}\) → 2
Example C
\(16^{\frac{1}{4}} = \sqrt[4]{16}\) → 2
Example D
\(x^{\frac{1}{5}} = \sqrt[5]{x}\)
8
More Fractional Indices
When the numerator is not 1: take the root and raise to the power.
\[ x^{\frac{m}{n}} = \left(\sqrt[n]{x}\right)^m \]
Strategy: root first, then power (keeps numbers smaller)
  • 1 \(27^{\frac{2}{3}} = \left(\sqrt[3]{27}\right)^2 = 3^2\) → \(9\)
  • 2 \(8^{\frac{2}{3}} = \left(\sqrt[3]{8}\right)^2 = 2^2\) → \(4\)
  • 3 \(16^{\frac{3}{4}} = \left(\sqrt[4]{16}\right)^3 = 2^3\) → \(8\)
  • 4 \(32^{\frac{3}{5}} = \left(\sqrt[5]{32}\right)^3 = 2^3\) → \(8\)
Tip: Always do the root first — it keeps the numbers smaller and easier to work with.
