1Geometry Theorems

Theorem 1 — Vertically Opposite Angles ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

Vertically opposite angles are equal in measure.

Two intersecting lines at O. Vertically opposite angles α = α and β = β.
Proof ★

Let two lines intersect at O, forming angles α, β, α′, β′.

α + β = 180° (angles on a straight line) β + α′ = 180° (angles on a straight line) ∴ α = 180° − β = α′ Similarly: β = β′
□
Theorem 2 — Isosceles Triangle ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

In an isosceles triangle, the angles opposite the equal sides are equal in measure. Conversely, if two angles are equal, the sides opposite them are equal.

Isosceles triangle ABC with |AB| = |AC| (tick marks). Base angles at B and C are equal.
Proof ★

Let triangle ABC have |AB| = |AC|. Construct the angle bisector from A to BC, meeting BC at D.

In triangles ABD and ACD:

|AB| = |AC| (given) |AD| = |AD| (common side) ∠BAD = ∠CAD (AD is angle bisector)

By SAS congruence: △ABD ≅ △ACD

Therefore: ∠ABD = ∠ACD, i.e. the base angles are equal.

□
Theorem 3 — Angle Sum in a Triangle ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

The angles in any triangle sum to 180°.

Line XAY drawn through A parallel to BC. Alternate angles are marked.
Proof ★

Draw line XAY through A parallel to BC.

∠XAB = ∠ABC = β (alternate angles, XY ∥ BC) ∠YAC = ∠ACB = γ (alternate angles, XY ∥ BC) β + α + γ = 180° (straight line at A)

∴ The angles of any triangle sum to 180°.

□
Theorem 4 — Exterior Angle of a Triangle ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

Each exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two interior opposite angles.

Proof ★

Let triangle ABC have an exterior angle ε at C (extend BC to D).

By Theorem 3: α + β + γ = 180° Straight line: γ + ε = 180° ∴ ε = 180° − γ = α + β
□
Diagram

Draw triangle ABC. Extend side BC beyond C to point D. The exterior angle ε = ∠ACD. Mark the two non-adjacent interior angles α (at A) and β (at B).

Theorem 5 — Parallelogram Properties ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

In a parallelogram: (a) opposite sides are equal, (b) opposite angles are equal, (c) the diagonals bisect each other.

Parallelogram ABCD with diagonals intersecting at E. Equal sides marked with tick marks.
Proof (a) & (b) — Opposite sides and angles ★

Draw diagonal AC. In triangles ABC and CDA:

∠BAC = ∠DCA (alternate angles, AB ∥ CD) ∠BCA = ∠DAC (alternate angles, AD ∥ BC) |AC| = |AC| (common)

By ASA: △ABC ≅ △CDA, giving |AB|=|CD|, |AD|=|BC|, ∠ABC=∠CDA.

□
Proof (c) — Diagonals bisect each other ★

Let diagonals meet at E. In triangles ABE and CDE:

|AB| = |CD| (proved above) ∠ABE = ∠CDE (alternate angles) ∠BAE = ∠DCE (alternate angles)

By ASA: △ABE ≅ △CDE, giving |AE|=|CE| and |BE|=|DE|.

□
Theorem 6 — Thales / Basic Proportionality Theorem
Theorem Statement

If a line is parallel to one side of a triangle, it divides the other two sides in the same ratio. Conversely, if a line divides two sides of a triangle in equal ratios, it is parallel to the third side.

Notes (Proof not required on LC HL)

If DE ∥ BC in triangle ABC (D on AB, E on AC):

|AD|/|DB| = |AE|/|EC| |AD|/|AB| = |AE|/|AC|

This theorem is fundamental to similar triangles and ratio problems.

Theorem 7 — Similar Triangles
Theorem Statement

If two triangles are equiangular (have the same three angles), they are similar and their corresponding sides are in proportion.

Notes (Proof not required on LC HL)
  • Similarity criteria: AA, SAS (sides in ratio, included angle equal), SSS (all sides in ratio).
  • If △ABC ∼ △DEF: |AB|/|DE| = |BC|/|EF| = |AC|/|DF|
  • Area ratio: (Area ABC)/(Area DEF) = (|AB|/|DE|)²
Theorem 8 — Pythagoras' Theorem ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

In a right-angled triangle, the square on the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares on the other two sides. If the right angle is at C: |AB|² = |AC|² + |BC|²

Right angle at C. Altitude CD dropped to hypotenuse AB. Three similar triangles: △ABC, △ACD, △CBD.
Proof (using similar triangles) ★

Drop perpendicular from C to AB, foot at D. Then △ABC ∼ △ACD ∼ △CBD.

From △ABC ∼ △ACD: |AC|² = |AB|·|AD| ...(i) From △ABC ∼ △CBD: |BC|² = |AB|·|BD| ...(ii) Adding: |AC|² + |BC|² = |AB|(|AD|+|BD|) = |AB|²
□
Notes

Converse: if |AB|² = |AC|² + |BC|², then the angle at C is 90°.

Theorem 9 — Angle in a Semicircle ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

The angle subtended at the circle by a diameter is a right angle (90°).

AB is a diameter. C is any point on the circle. ∠ACB = 90°. O is the centre; OA = OB = OC (radii).
Proof ★

Let |OA|=|OB|=|OC| (radii). Triangles OAC and OBC are isosceles.

Let ∠OAC = ∠OCA = α (isosceles △OAC) Let ∠OBC = ∠OCB = β (isosceles △OBC) Angle sum in △ABC: α + (α + β) + β = 180° 2α + 2β = 180° ∠ACB = α + β = 90°
□
Theorem 10 — Tangent Perpendicular to Radius ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

A tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency.

Proof by Contradiction ★

Let T be the point of tangency on circle with centre O, and tangent line L.

Suppose OT is not perpendicular to L. Then there exists a point P on L with |OP| < |OT|.

But P is outside the circle (L is a tangent, meets circle only at T), so |OP| > radius = |OT|.

This contradicts |OP| < |OT|. ∴ OT must be perpendicular to L.

□
Theorem 11 — Equal Tangents from External Point ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

Tangent segments drawn from an external point to a circle are equal in length.

Proof ★

Let PA and PB be tangents from P, touching at A and B. Draw OA, OB, OP.

In triangles OAP and OBP:

|OA| = |OB| (radii) ∠OAP = ∠OBP = 90° (tangent ⊥ radius, Thm 10) |OP| = |OP| (common hypotenuse)

By RHS congruence: △OAP ≅ △OBP, so |PA| = |PB|.

□

2Trigonometry Theorems

The Sine Rule ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

In any triangle ABC with sides a = |BC|, b = |CA|, c = |AB|:

a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C
Proof ★

Draw altitude h from C to AB:

In △ACH: sin A = h/b → h = b sin A In △BCH: sin B = h/a → h = a sin B Therefore: b sin A = a sin B a/sin A = b/sin B ...(i) Similarly (altitude from A): b/sin B = c/sin C ...(ii) Combining: a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C
□
When to use the Sine Rule
  • AAS or ASA cases — two angles and one side known
  • SSA — the ambiguous case (may have 0, 1 or 2 solutions)
The Cosine Rule ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement
a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A cos A = (b² + c² − a²) / (2bc)
Proof (coordinate geometry) ★

Place A at origin, B = (c, 0), C = (b cos A, b sin A).

a² = |BC|² = (b cos A − c)² + (b sin A)² = b²cos²A − 2bc cos A + c² + b²sin²A = b²(cos²A + sin²A) + c² − 2bc cos A = b² + c² − 2bc cos A
□
When to use the Cosine Rule
  • SAS — two sides and included angle
  • SSS — all three sides known
  • Special case: A = 90° gives Pythagoras (cos 90° = 0)
Area of a Triangle
Formula
Area = ½ bc sin A

where b and c are two sides and A is the included angle.

Proof
Height h from C to AB: h = b sin A Area = ½ × base × height = ½ × c × b sin A = ½bc sin A
□
Compound Angle Formulae ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement
sin(A+B) = sinA cosB + cosA sinB sin(A−B) = sinA cosB − cosA sinB cos(A+B) = cosA cosB − sinA sinB cos(A−B) = cosA cosB + sinA sinB tan(A+B) = (tanA + tanB) / (1 − tanA tanB)
Proof of cos(A+B) ★

Place unit circle. Let P = (cos A, sin A), Q = (cos B, −sin B).

|PQ|² = (cosA − cosB)² + (sinA + sinB)² = 2 − 2cosA cosB + 2sinA sinB ...(i) By cosine rule on △OPQ (|OP|=|OQ|=1, angle = A+B): |PQ|² = 2 − 2cos(A+B) ...(ii) Equating: cos(A+B) = cosA cosB − sinA sinB
□
Deriving sin(A+B) from cos(A+B)
sin(A+B) = cos(90°−(A+B)) = cos((90°−A)−B) = cos(90°−A)cosB + sin(90°−A)sinB = sinA cosB + cosA sinB
Double Angle Formulae (set B = A)
sin 2A = 2 sin A cos A cos 2A = cos²A − sin²A = 2cos²A − 1 = 1 − 2sin²A tan 2A = 2tanA / (1 − tan²A)

3Algebra & Complex Numbers

Factor Theorem ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

If f(x) is a polynomial, then (x − a) is a factor of f(x) if and only if f(a) = 0.

Proof ★

By the Polynomial Division Algorithm:

f(x) = (x−a)·q(x) + r (r is a constant) Substitute x = a: f(a) = (a−a)·q(a) + r = r (⇒) If f(a) = 0, then r = 0, so (x−a) is a factor. (⇐) If (x−a) is a factor, then r = 0, so f(a) = 0.
□
Proof by Mathematical Induction ★ PROOF
The Principle

To prove P(n) for all n ≥ 1: prove P(1) (base case), then prove P(k) ⟹ P(k+1) (inductive step).

Example: Sum of first n natural numbers ★

Claim: 1 + 2 + 3 + ··· + n = n(n+1)/2

Base case (n=1): LHS = 1, RHS = 1(2)/2 = 1 ✓ Inductive step: Assume 1+2+···+k = k(k+1)/2 1+2+···+k+(k+1) = k(k+1)/2 + (k+1) = (k+1)[k/2 + 1] = (k+1)(k+2)/2 = (k+1)((k+1)+1)/2 ✓
True for all n ∈ ℕ by induction. □
Example: Sum of squares ★

Claim: 1² + 2² + ··· + n² = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6

Base case (n=1): LHS = 1, RHS = 1(2)(3)/6 = 1 ✓ Inductive step: Assume true for k. Then: 1² + ··· + k² + (k+1)² = k(k+1)(2k+1)/6 + (k+1)² = (k+1)[k(2k+1)/6 + (k+1)] = (k+1)(2k²+7k+6)/6 = (k+1)(k+2)(2k+3)/6 = (k+1)((k+1)+1)(2(k+1)+1)/6 ✓
□
Example: Divisibility ★

Claim: 3ⁿ − 1 is divisible by 2 for all n ≥ 1.

Base case: 3¹ − 1 = 2 = 2(1) ✓ Assume 3ᵏ − 1 = 2m. Then: 3^(k+1) − 1 = 3·3ᵏ − 1 = 3(2m+1) − 1 = 6m+2 = 2(3m+1) ✓
□
De Moivre's Theorem ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement
(cos θ + i sin θ)ⁿ = cos nθ + i sin nθ for all n ∈ ℤ
Proof by Induction ★
Base case (n=1): (cosθ + i sinθ)¹ = cos θ + i sin θ ✓ Inductive step: Assume (cosθ + i sinθ)ᵏ = cos kθ + i sin kθ (cosθ + i sinθ)^(k+1) = (cos kθ + i sin kθ)(cos θ + i sin θ) = cos kθ cosθ − sin kθ sinθ + i(sin kθ cosθ + cos kθ sinθ) = cos(kθ+θ) + i sin(kθ+θ) [compound angle formulae] = cos(k+1)θ + i sin(k+1)θ ✓
□
Applications
  • Powers of complex numbers in polar form
  • Finding nth roots of complex numbers
  • Expressing cos nθ, sin nθ in terms of cos θ, sin θ
  • Roots of unity: solutions of zⁿ = 1 are e^(2πki/n), k = 0,1,...,n−1

4Calculus Theorems

Definition of the Derivative (First Principles) ★ PROOF
Definition
f′(x) = lim[h→0] [f(x+h) − f(x)] / h
Proof: d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹ ★
f′(x) = lim[h→0] [(x+h)ⁿ − xⁿ] / h Expand (x+h)ⁿ by Binomial Theorem: = lim[h→0] [nxⁿ⁻¹h + C(n,2)xⁿ⁻²h² + ... + hⁿ] / h = lim[h→0] [nxⁿ⁻¹ + C(n,2)xⁿ⁻²h + ... + hⁿ⁻¹] = nxⁿ⁻¹
□
Proof: d/dx(sin x) = cos x ★
f′(x) = lim[h→0] [sin(x+h) − sin x] / h Using sin(x+h) = sinx cosh + cosx sinh: = lim[h→0] [sinx(cosh−1) + cosx sinh] / h = sinx · lim(cosh−1)/h + cosx · lim sinh/h = sinx · 0 + cosx · 1 = cos x

Standard limits used: lim[h→0] sinh/h = 1 and lim[h→0] (cosh−1)/h = 0

□
Proof: d/dx(eˣ) = eˣ
f′(x) = lim[h→0] [e^(x+h) − eˣ] / h = eˣ · lim[h→0] (eʰ−1)/h = eˣ · 1 = eˣ

Standard limit used: lim[h→0] (eʰ−1)/h = 1

□
Product Rule ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement
d/dx[u·v] = u′v + uv′
Proof from first principles ★
y = uv y + δy = (u+δu)(v+δv) = uv + u·δv + v·δu + δu·δv δy = u·δv + v·δu + δu·δv δy/δx = u(δv/δx) + v(δu/δx) + δu(δv/δx) As δx → 0: δu → 0 dy/dx = u(dv/dx) + v(du/dx) = uv′ + vu′
□
Quotient Rule & Chain Rule
Quotient Rule
d/dx[u/v] = (u′v − uv′) / v²
Chain Rule
If y = f(g(x)), then dy/dx = f′(g(x))·g′(x) Or: dy/dx = (dy/du)·(du/dx)
Note

The chain rule proof is not on the LC HL examined proof list but must be applied. Intuition: "differentiate outside × differentiate inside".

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
Theorem Statement

If F′(x) = f(x), then:

∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx = F(b) − F(a)
Notes on Integration
  • Integration by substitution: if u = g(x), then du = g′(x) dx
  • Integration by parts: ∫u dv = uv − ∫v du
  • Riemann Sum: ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx = lim[n→∞] Σ f(xᵢ)Δx

5Number Theory

Infinitely Many Primes (Euclid) ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

There are infinitely many prime numbers.

Proof by Contradiction ★

Suppose there are finitely many primes: p₁, p₂, ..., pₙ.

Let N = (p₁ · p₂ · ··· · pₙ) + 1 Case 1: N is prime. → N is not in our list — contradiction. Case 2: N is composite. → N has a prime factor p. → p ≠ any pᵢ (dividing N by any pᵢ leaves remainder 1) → p is a prime not in our list — contradiction. In both cases: contradiction. ∴ There are infinitely many primes.
□
√2 is Irrational ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement

√2 cannot be expressed as p/q where p, q ∈ ℤ, q ≠ 0.

Proof by Contradiction ★
Suppose √2 = p/q in lowest terms (gcd(p,q) = 1). Then: 2 = p²/q² → p² = 2q² So p² is even → p is even. Write p = 2k. (2k)² = 2q² → 4k² = 2q² → q² = 2k² So q² is even → q is even. But then gcd(p,q) ≥ 2, contradicting our assumption. ∴ √2 is irrational.
□
Quadratic Formula ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement
For ax² + bx + c = 0 (a ≠ 0): x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) / (2a)
Proof by Completing the Square ★
ax² + bx + c = 0 x² + (b/a)x + c/a = 0 (x + b/(2a))² = b²/(4a²) − c/a (x + b/(2a))² = (b²−4ac)/(4a²) x + b/(2a) = ±√(b²−4ac) / (2a) x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) / (2a)
□
The Discriminant Δ = b² − 4ac
  • Δ > 0 : two distinct real roots
  • Δ = 0 : one repeated root (x = −b/2a)
  • Δ < 0 : no real roots (two complex conjugate roots)

6Sequences & Series

Geometric Series Formula ★ PROOF
Formula
Sₙ = a(1 − rⁿ)/(1 − r) for r ≠ 1 S∞ = a/(1 − r) for |r| < 1
Proof ★
Sₙ = a + ar + ar² + ... + arⁿ⁻¹ ...(i) rSₙ = ar + ar² + ... + arⁿ⁻¹ + arⁿ ...(ii) Subtract (ii) from (i): Sₙ(1−r) = a − arⁿ = a(1−rⁿ) Sₙ = a(1−rⁿ)/(1−r) For |r| < 1, as n → ∞: rⁿ → 0 S∞ = a/(1−r)
□
Arithmetic Series Formula ★ PROOF
Formula
Sₙ = (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d) = (n/2)(a + l)
Proof (Gauss method) ★
Sₙ = a + (a+d) + (a+2d) + ... + l Sₙ = l + (l−d) + (l−2d) + ... + a Adding: 2Sₙ = n(a + l) Sₙ = (n/2)(a + l) = (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d)
□

7Probability & Statistics

Binomial Theorem ★ PROOF
Theorem Statement
(a+b)ⁿ = Σ C(n,k) aⁿ⁻ᵏ bᵏ (k = 0 to n) C(n,k) = n! / (k!(n−k)!)
Pascal's Identity (used in proof)
C(n,r) = C(n−1,r−1) + C(n−1,r) Proof: C(n−1,r−1) + C(n−1,r) = (n−1)!/((r−1)!(n−r)!) + (n−1)!/(r!(n−1−r)!) = (n−1)![r + (n−r)] / (r!(n−r)!) = n! / (r!(n−r)!) = C(n,r) □
Proof by Induction ★
Base cases: n=0: (a+b)⁰=1 ✓; n=1: (a+b)¹=a+b ✓ Inductive step: Assume true for n=k. (a+b)^(k+1) = (a+b)·Σ C(k,j)aᵏ⁻ʲbʲ = Σ C(k,j)aᵏ⁺¹⁻ʲbʲ + Σ C(k,j)aᵏ⁻ʲbʲ⁺¹ Re-indexing and using Pascal's Identity: = Σ C(k+1,j)aᵏ⁺¹⁻ʲbʲ (j=0 to k+1) ✓
□
Pascal's Triangle
Row 0: 1 Row 1: 1 1 Row 2: 1 2 1 Row 3: 1 3 3 1 Row 4: 1 4 6 4 1

Each entry is the sum of the two entries above it (Pascal's Identity).

Central Limit Theorem (Statement only)
Theorem Statement

If X₁, X₂, ..., Xₙ are independent random variables with mean μ and variance σ², then for large n, the sample mean X̄ is approximately normally distributed:

X̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n) approximately

regardless of the distribution of the individual Xᵢ.

Notes
  • Rule of thumb: n ≥ 30 for the normal approximation
  • 95% Confidence Interval: X̄ ± 1.96 · (σ/√n)
  • The proof (using moment generating functions) is not required
