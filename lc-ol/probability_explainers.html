Part 1

What is Probability?

The Big Idea

Probability is a way of measuring how likely something is to happen. We use a number between 0 and 1 to describe it.

❌
Impossible → 0

It cannot happen. Probability = 0.

✅
Certain → 1

It will definitely happen. Probability = 1.

🪙
Even Chance → ½

Equally likely to happen or not happen.

📊
Everything else

Falls somewhere between 0 and 1.

The Probability Scale

Impossible Unlikely Even Chance Likely Certain

The Basic Formula

P(event) = Number of favourable outcomes Total number of equally likely outcomes

Worked Examples

Describing events as Impossible, Even Chance, or Certain

Question Describe each event as Impossible, Even Chance, or Certain:
  1. You roll a normal die and get a number less than 10.
  2. You flip a fair coin and it lands on heads.
  3. You pick a card from a normal deck and it is a joker. (Standard decks have no jokers.)
  4. January will have 31 days next year.
  5. You roll a normal die and get a 7.
(a) A normal die shows 1–6. All of those are less than 10. It must happen. Certain
(b) A fair coin has two sides — heads or tails. Each is equally likely. Even Chance
(c) A standard 52-card deck contains no jokers. It cannot happen. Impossible
(d) January always has 31 days — it never changes. Certain
(e) A normal die only goes up to 6. Getting a 7 is not possible. Impossible
2

Placing events on a 7-label probability scale

Question The scale has seven labels: Impossible, Very unlikely, Unlikely, Even Chance, Likely, Very likely, Certain.

Choose the best label for each event:
  1. You will score 6 on a single roll of a normal die.
  2. It will be dark outside at midnight tonight.
  3. You will draw a heart from a shuffled deck of 52 cards.
  4. You toss a coin — it shows tails.
  5. You pick a month at random and it starts with the letter J.
  6. A player scores on a penalty kick in soccer.
  7. You roll a die and get an odd number.
(a) Only 1 out of 6 outcomes is a 6. Low chance. Unlikely
(b) It is always dark at midnight. This is certain. Certain
(c) 13 hearts out of 52 cards = ¼ chance. Less than even, more than very unlikely. Unlikely
(d) Heads or tails — exactly 50/50. Even Chance
(e) 3 months start with J (January, June, July) out of 12. P = 3/12 = ¼ — lower than evens. Unlikely
(f) Professional penalty kicks are scored most of the time — well above evens. Very likely
(g) 3 odd numbers (1, 3, 5) out of 6 = ½. That's exactly even. Even Chance
💡 Key tip: When you're not sure, try to work out a fraction first. If it equals ½ → Even Chance. Less than ½ → Unlikely side. More than ½ → Likely side. Close to 0 → Very unlikely. Close to 1 → Very likely.

She turns over another card. Answer these questions:
  1. Is it more likely the next card shows an even number or an odd number? Explain.
  2. Is it more likely the next card shows a number greater than 6 or less than 6? Explain.

Working it out:

After removing the 6, the remaining 8 cards are: 2, 10, 3, 8, 1, 12, 5, 9

(i) Even or Odd?

Even numbers left: 2, 10, 8, 12 → 4 cards
Odd numbers left: 3, 1, 5, 9 → 4 cards
P(even) = 4/8 = ½    P(odd) = 4/8 = ½
✅ Both are equally likely — it's an even chance either way.

(ii) Greater than 6 or Less than 6?

Greater than 6: 10, 8, 12, 9 → 4 cards
Less than 6: 2, 3, 1, 5 → 4 cards
P(>6) = 4/8 = ½    P(<6) = 4/8 = ½
✅ Again, both equally likely — even chance.

⚠️ Important: Once a card is turned over and removed, always count what's left. Here we started with 9 cards, one was removed, so 8 remain.

Placing arrows on a probability scale

Question A bag contains beads:
4 red, 2 blue, 1 green, 1 white → 8 beads total
One bead is taken out without looking. Place an arrow on the probability scale to show the probability of:
  1. picking a red bead
  2. picking a blue bead
  3. picking a white bead
  4. picking a yellow bead

Calculating each probability:

(i) Red: 4 out of 8 → P = 4/8 = ½ Exactly half the beads are red → even chance.
(ii) Blue: 2 out of 8 → P = 2/8 = ¼ Less than ½ → unlikely (but not very unlikely).
(iii) White: 1 out of 8 → P = 1/8 Low probability → unlikely, closer to impossible than ½.
(iv) Yellow: 0 out of 8 → P = 0 There are no yellow beads in the bag → Impossible.

Reading spinners — comparing probability of colours

Question Here are three spinners. Each is divided into equal sections.

Spinner A
(2 red, 1 blue, 1 yellow)

Spinner B
(4 green, 1 red, 1 blue)

Spinner C
(1 red, 1 yellow, 1 blue)

  1. Which spinner has an even chance of stopping on red?
  2. Which spinner is most likely to stop on green?
  3. What is the probability of Spinner C stopping on yellow?
(i) Even chance of red?

Spinner A: P(red) = 2/4 = ½ ✅
Spinner B: P(red) = 1/6 — too low.
Spinner C: P(red) = 1/3 — less than ½.
Answer: Spinner A

(ii) Most likely to stop on green?

Only Spinner B has green — and 4 out of 6 sections are green.
P(green on B) = 4/6 = ⅔. Answer: Spinner B

(iii) P(yellow on C)?

Spinner C has 3 equal sections. Yellow takes up 1 section.
P(yellow) = 1/3
